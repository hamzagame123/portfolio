DESIGN CHALLENGES

UX Obstacles & Solutions

Key design challenges and how I addressed them

01

Building Trust Between Strangers

Challenge

Users hesitate to buy food from unknown farmers; trust is earned slowly but lost instantly

Design Solution

Multi-layered trust signals: verified badges, star ratings, review counts, farm photos, "years farming" stat, and direct messaging before purchase

02

Making Sustainability Tangible

Challenge

"Eco-friendly" feels abstract; users can't see or feel reduced carbon emissions

Design Solution

Quantified impact: "You saved 2.3kg COâ‚‚" on checkout, cumulative stats in profile, gamified "Earth Guardian" levels with progress bars

03

Serving Two User Types

Challenge

Farmers and buyers have fundamentally different needs, but both must use the same app

Design Solution

Buyer-focused MVP with farmer-friendly features: easy product upload (future), dashboard view, order management, and customer messaging

04

Information Density Balance

Challenge

Users need rich product info (farm, distance, price, organic status) without overwhelming mobile screens

Design Solution

Progressive disclosure: cards show essentials, detail view reveals depth. Visual badges reduce text. Category emojis enable quick scanning

Trust Is the Product

In a marketplace connecting strangers, every design decision must answer: "Does this make users feel safe?" Verified badges, ratings, and messaging aren't just features; they're the foundation.

ðŸŽ¯

Quantify the Abstract

"Sustainable" means nothing until it's "2.3kg COâ‚‚ saved." Turning eco-impact into numbers makes it real, shareable, and motivating. Gamification (Earth Guardian levels) adds emotional reward.

ðŸŽ¨

Design Signals Quality

Users judge reliability by aesthetics before functionality. The polished green theme and Fraunces typography signal "premium" and "trustworthy," which is critical for food purchases from strangers.

ðŸ”„

Two Users, One Vision

Designing for both farmers and buyers required understanding their opposing pain points, then finding features that solve both: direct messaging helps buyers trust AND helps farmers explain.

REFLECTION

Personal Takeaways

This project taught me that sustainable design isn't just about environmental impact. It's about creating systems that sustain trust, relationships, and communities. The Locavore movement isn't just about food miles; it's about reconnecting people with their food sources and each other.

The most challenging aspect was designing for two fundamentally different user types with opposing needs. Farmers need efficient tools to list products and manage orders; buyers need confidence-building features and easy discovery. Finding the overlap (direct messaging, verified profiles, transparent pricing) was the key insight.

If I were to start over, I would conduct more in-depth farmer interviews earlier in the process. While buyer needs are well-documented in market research, farmer pain points around technology adoption and digital literacy would benefit from more ethnographic research.
